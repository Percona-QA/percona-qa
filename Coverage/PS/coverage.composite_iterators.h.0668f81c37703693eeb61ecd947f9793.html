26 /**
27 @file composite_iterators.h
28
29 A composite row iterator is one that takes in one or more existing iterators
30 and processes their rows in some interesting way. They are usually not bound
31 to a single table or similar, but are the inner (non-leaf) nodes of the
32 iterator execution tree. They consistently own their source iterator, although
33 not its memory (since we never allocate row iterators on the heap--usually on
34 a MEM_ROOT>). This means that in the end, you'll end up with a single root
35 iterator which then owns everything else recursively.
36
37 SortingIterator and the two window iterators are also composite iterators,
38 but are defined in their own files.
39 */

627 /**
628 Like semijoin materialization, weedout works on the basic idea that a semijoin
629 is just like an inner join as we long as we can get rid of the duplicates
630 somehow. (This is advantageous, because inner joins can be reordered, whereas
631 semijoins generally can't.) However, unlike semijoin materialization, weedout
632 removes duplicates after the join, not before it. Consider something like
633
634 SELECT * FROM t1 WHERE a IN ( SELECT b FROM t2 );
635
636 Semijoin materialization solves this by materializing t2, with deduplication,
637 and then joining. Weedout joins t1 to t2 and then leaves only one output row
638 per t1 row. The disadvantage is that this potentially needs to discard more
639 rows; the (potential) advantage is that we deduplicate on t1 instead of t2.
640
641 Weedout, unlike materialization, works in a streaming fashion; rows are output
642 (or discarded) as they come in, with a temporary table used for recording the
643 row IDs we've seen before. (We need to deduplicate on t1's row IDs, not its
644 contents.) See SJ_TMP_TABLE for details about the table format.
645 */
